In a statement that's sending shockwaves through the advertising industry, Amy Lanzi, CEO of Digitas North America, didn't mince words: **"CMO is a dying role. And it should be."**

Speaking live at the Cannes Lion Advertising Festival with The Verge's Nilay Patel, Lanzi delivered a blunt assessment of where she sees the traditional chief marketing officer heading—and why that's actually a good thing.

## The Death of the Traditional CMO

The comment landed hard in a room full of marketing executives. But Lanzi wasn't being provocative for its own sake. Her argument is rooted in how modern business works: **marketing must drive commercial value and shareholder value**, or it risks becoming irrelevant.

"The previous version of the CMO, sure," Lanzi clarified. "Because if they're not [driving value], just like the whole—we are in a chaotic world and all the rise, we're in an area of convergence. So if you are a CMO and you're not thinking about your data layer, you're not thinking about your full stacks, you can get closer to consumer and get to better creative, better use of creators, you are not going to be doing well in the future."

The traditional CMO role—responsible for building campaigns and moving on—is over. In its place, Lanzi sees the rise of the **Chief Growth Officer (CGO)**, a role that treats marketing as a business function rather than a creative exercise.

## AI Is the New Programmatic (Again)

Lanzi drew a sharp parallel between the current AI hype cycle and the rise of programmatic advertising a decade ago.

"I always go back to the promise of programmatic," she said. "How many of you remember when programmatic was a thing? No more people. It was all going to magically… And it magically still needs people, still has the nuance of brands and the marketplace."

Her agency, Digitas, actually released a satirical ad called "The Wrong Promises" before Cannes, calling out the wild claims being made about AI in advertising pitches—from free AI platforms to guaranteed results.

"We're seeing all kinds of partners offer wild things in the pitch process," Lanzi explained. "Insane commercial deals that are just generally bad for people and the business."

## What AI Is Actually Good For

Despite her skepticism about AI hype, Lanzi is far from anti-AI. Digitas launched **Digitas AI** two years ago, and the approach has been decidedly practical:

"We started to make our people better unicorns. Everyone look at what's on your desk, like what's in your day, and think about what you absolutely could build an agent to do. And that way we free up our time to do this."

The key, according to Lanzi, is using AI to **enhance human creativity rather than replace it**—a point she illustrated with a Virgin Cruises case study where AI helped teams move from concept to execution faster, but the core ideas still came from human brains.

## The New Marketing Organization

To adapt, Digitas restructured with three new C-suite roles:

- **Chief Intelligence Officer**: Lifted from data and analytics to build an intelligence platform that all agents run on
- **Chief Systems Officer**: Redesigned from the COO role to focus on marketing systems and client tech stacks
- **Chief Transformation Officer**: Specifically for helping CMOs transform into CGOs

## What This Means for Marketing Leaders

Lanzi's message is clear: the era of the CMO who "was responsible for building these campaigns and then moving on" is over. Marketing leaders must become **architects of growth systems**—integrating data layers, AI tools, creator partnerships, and commerce capabilities into unified engines that drive measurable business results.

The traditional CMO may be dying. But the marketing leaders who evolve will be more valuable than ever.
