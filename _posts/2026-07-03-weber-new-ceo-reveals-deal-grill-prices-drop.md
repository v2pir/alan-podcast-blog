Roger Dahle didn't plan to become the king of American grilling. He started Blackstone in 2008, building flat-top griddles that nobody really noticed — until the pandemic hit and smash burgers went viral on TikTok. Suddenly, everyone wanted a Blackstone.

Fast forward to today: Dahle is now CEO of Weber Blackstone, the combined company that resulted from his acquisition of Weber, the iconic 70-year-old grill company. In a wide-ranging interview on *Decoder*, Dahle revealed exactly how the merger happened, why it's been harder than expected to merge two very different company cultures, and what's coming next for American grill buyers.

## The Deal That Got Stuck in Bureaucracy

The Blackstone-Weber deal almost didn't happen — and not because of market forces.

"We signed our agreement in December, and that's when the clock started ticking with the FTC," Dahle explained. "They have five commissioners in Washington, D.C., and they needed all five to be in place. As soon as they were fully staffed, they called us up and said, yeah, your deal's fine. Go ahead."

The FTC's review took months, delaying the integration process. "It just took a minute," Dahle said. "I think we were just in the middle of transition with administration."

## Two Companies, Two Completely Different Cultures

The hardest part of the merger hasn't been products or manufacturing — it's been culture.

"Whenever you get a company that's been around 70-plus years, you start getting too structured, you start getting too layered, and you start getting too siloed," Dahle said. "That's completely opposite of the way we do things at Blackstone."

At Blackstone, Dahle runs an entrepreneurial operation where employees are encouraged to pick up trash in the parking lot — literally. At Weber, he discovered something different: employees were afraid to expand beyond their defined roles "because they fear that they might be taking the janitor's job away."

The integration is still ongoing. "We're still in that process," Dahle admitted. "It's going to take me another year to fully get that done."

## Why Consumers Are Trading Down — Big Time

Here's the part that should worry every premium grill manufacturer: customers are abandoning expensive grills in favor of cheaper options.

"50% of our consumers are spending 15% to 20% on power — that includes electricity at home, gasoline, propane," Dahle revealed. "If they're spending 50% of their income on housing and 20, 25% on power, they have no money left. It's a really difficult economy for them right now. So yeah, they're trading down to charcoal grills because of price point."

This isn't a small trend. It's a fundamental shift in who can afford to grill. Dahle noted that people aren't just switching from a 36-inch griddle to a 28-inch — they're going "all the way down to a charcoal grill."

## The Tariff Pain Is Real — But There's a Twist

Every grill company complains about tariffs. But Dahle offered a surprising perspective on whether they're actually working as intended.

"The theory of the tariffs is they'll bring up the prices of the overseas products to the same as the prices that you would have to charge if you made them in the United States," he said. "It doesn't sound like that's actually pushing you to manufacture more in the United States."

Weber still builds most of its iconic kettles in Huntley, Illinois — a decision that's become both a point of pride and a pricing challenge. "We upgraded the quality of that thing. It has a 10-year warranty on it. That's amazing," Dahle said. "But customers vote with their credit card and they vote for $300 because the quality is the same."

## The One Marketing Channel That Still Beats Everything

Despite spending millions on TV, social media, and influencer partnerships, Dahle admitted something surprising: word of mouth is still his biggest customer acquisition channel.

"When we do surveys annually, which I've done now for about seven years, still the number one way people hear about our brand — it's still by word of mouth," he said.

That authentic recommendation matters more than paid influence. "When you buy and pay for influencers, the consumer picks it out in seconds," Dahle said. "The consumer is so savvy and so smart."

## What's Next for Weber Blackstone

Dahle is now focused on bringing Blackstone's fast-paced product development culture to Weber's more deliberate approach. "I can't take three years to get a new product in a box. I got to take three months," he said.

He's also looking at price points. The classic Weber kettle has crept up from a traditional $100 retail item to $150-$200. "What can we do innovatively to help the customer have a better experience at a better price point?" Dahle asked.

The answer might involve more U.S. manufacturing — but only if the economics work. "I'd love to build more in the U.S. if I could, but the customer votes with their credit card."
